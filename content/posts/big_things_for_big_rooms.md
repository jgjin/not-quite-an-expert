[Some psychology research](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2017-26534-001) suggests people tend to see larger works of art as better.[^1] Large-scale works can evoke a sense of awe or importance. Size is such an important visual attribute that [we process size pre-attentively](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12292122/#sec2-bioengineering-12-00782). However, [some additional psychology research](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2002-17694-002) suggests people can misattribute their preferences to meanings rather than size. 
[^1]: However, note [many psychology studies fail to reproduce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Replication_crisis#In_psychology).

To my friends who describe themselves as not liking museums I suggest we go to the Metropolitan Museum, which houses such large-scale works as [the Temple of Dendur](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/547802) and [the choir screen from the Cathedral of Valladolid](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/201926). A recent exhibit at the Hirshhorn Museum, ["Big Things for Big Rooms"](https://hirshhorn.si.edu/exhibitions/big-things-for-big-rooms) takes this idea to the extreme. Sure, this pile of rocks seems basic. However, it's a large pile of rocks in a museum, so it must be important.
